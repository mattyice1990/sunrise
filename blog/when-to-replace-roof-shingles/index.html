Seven Telltale Signs Your Shingles Are Failing

Knowing when to replace your shingles is a lot like learning to listen to your house. Your roof sends signalsâ€”visible cues that tell you if it's healthy, just getting old, or in real trouble. Spotting these signs early can be the difference between a straightforward re-roofing project and dealing with major interior damage down the road.

Learning to recognize these seven critical warnings will help you determine if your roof needs a simple repair or is nearing the end of its functional life.

1. Curling or Clawing Shingle Edges

One of the most obvious red flags is when shingle edges start to curl up, or the middle of the shingle begins to buckle and lift. Think of it like a piece of paper left out in the sunâ€”over time, the heat and UV exposure dry out the asphalt, causing it to shrink and deform.

Curled shingles are a serious problem. They break the watertight seal thatâ€™s supposed to keep your home dry, creating an easy target for high winds to catch, lift, and tear them right off the roof. Once that happens, your underlayment is exposed and vulnerable to rain.

2. Cracks and Bald Spots

Asphalt shingles get brittle with age. The daily cycle of heating up and cooling downâ€”what we call thermal shockâ€”causes stress that eventually leads to visible cracks. Don't underestimate them; these cracks are direct pathways for water to seep through to the roof decking, where it can cause rot.

You might also notice "bald spots" where the sand-like granules have completely worn away. Those granules are the shingle's first line of defense against the elements. Once they're gone, the asphalt underneath is exposed, and the shingle's deterioration speeds up dramatically.

3. Granules in Your Gutters

Finding a pile of shingle granules in your gutters is a classic sign of an aging roof. It's normal for a brand-new roof to shed some of its extra granules, but if your roof is over 10-15 years old, a significant accumulation is a clear warning of advanced wear.

Think of those granules as the tread on a tire. When they wear off, your roof loses its grip and its protection, leaving the underlying asphalt exposed to UV rays that make it brittle and weak.

4. Missing or Damaged Shingles

This one is usually easy to spot from the ground. High winds from a storm can rip shingles right off, leaving obvious gaps in your roof's armor. These exposed spots are immediate entry points for water.

Even a single missing shingle can cause a serious leak, which is why a prompt roof repair in Tucson is so critical. Addressing a small problem now prevents it from turning into a massive, expensive one later.

5. A Sagging Roof Deck

If you notice a droop, dip, or wavy spot in your roofline, this is one of the most serious signs of trouble you can see. A sagging roof almost always points to a structural issue with the decking underneath, typically caused by trapped moisture that has led to rot.

This isn't something to ignore. A sagging roof can signal a risk of collapse, so it's essential to call a professional immediately.

6. Dark Streaks or Algae Growth

Those ugly black or dark green streaks you see on roofs, especially in more humid climates, are actually a type of airborne algae called Gloeocapsa magma. While it starts as a cosmetic issue, over time, the algae can trap moisture against the shingles and accelerate their decay.

Moss is even worse. It acts like a sponge, holding a significant amount of water against the roof surface. As it grows, it can lift the edges of shingles, breaking their seal and allowing water to sneak underneath.

7. Your Roof Is Getting Old

Sometimes, the biggest sign is simply time. Even if a roof looks okay from a distance, age is a major factor. Most asphalt shingle roofs start showing clear signs of wear and tear after 15-20 years of service.

According to the Insurance Institute for Business & Home Safety (IBHS), roofs that are 15 years or older are far more likely to fail during severe weather like hailstorms or high winds. Waiting until you see a leak is often waiting too long.

For homes in colder climates, the most damaging force is the freeze-thaw cycle. Itâ€™s a quiet, subtle destroyer that works from the inside out.
