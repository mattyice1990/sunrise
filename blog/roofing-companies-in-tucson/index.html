How to Check a Roofer's License

In Arizona, roofing is a serious, licensed trade. The Arizona Registrar of Contractors (ROC) is the agency that keeps everyone honest. Any job totaling more than $1,000‚Äîthat‚Äôs materials and labor‚Äîlegally requires a licensed contractor.

Don‚Äôt just take their word for it. You have to verify it yourself. Luckily, the ROC makes it incredibly easy.

  1. Get their ROC License Number. Any legit roofer will have this printed on their truck, business card, and estimates. If they hesitate to give it to you, that's a huge red flag.
  2. Go to the ROC website. Pull up the official Arizona Registrar of Contractors site on your phone or computer.
  3. Use the "Contractor Search." The best way is to search by the license number for a direct hit, but you can also search by the company name.

In about 30 seconds, you'll see if their license is active, expired, or worse, suspended. This search also reveals any formal complaints filed against them, giving you a real look into their track record.

How to Understand Roofing Warranties

A warranty is way more than a piece of paper you file away. It‚Äôs the roofer‚Äôs promise‚Äîa direct measure of their confidence in their materials and, more importantly, their own installation quality. When you're comparing quotes from Tucson roofing companies, knowing the difference between the two main types of warranties is critical for protecting your investment.

It‚Äôs easy to get lost in the fine print, but it really boils down to two separate promises: one from the factory that made the shingles, and one from the crew that nailed them to your house. Confusing the two can lead to some serious headaches and unexpected bills down the road.

Manufacturer Warranty Versus Workmanship Warranty

First up is the manufacturer‚Äôs warranty. This guarantee comes straight from the company that produced the shingles, tiles, or coating. It only covers defects in the materials themselves. For example, if a bad batch of shingles starts curling or losing granules way too early because of a flaw at the factory, this is the warranty that kicks in.

The workmanship warranty, on the other hand, is the guarantee from the roofing contractor you hire. Honestly, this is the one that matters most for homeowners. It covers mistakes made during the installation‚Äîthings like improperly installed flashing, wrong nailing patterns, or bad sealing. The hard truth is that most roof failures, especially in the first few years, come from installation errors, not bad materials.

A strong, long-term workmanship warranty from a solid local company is your best protection. It proves the contractor backs their own skill and will show up to fix any installation mistakes, which are the #1 source of leaks.

A Real-World Scenario: What If a Leak Appears?

Picture this: Two years after your roof was replaced, a big monsoon storm hits. Suddenly, you see a water stain spreading across your ceiling. Who do you call? This is where the warranty type makes all the difference.

  • If the leak is from a faulty shingle that cracked for no apparent reason, the manufacturer's warranty covers the cost of the new shingle. But here's the catch: it often does not cover the labor to tear off the bad one and install the replacement.
  • If the leak is caused by poorly sealed flashing around a vent pipe, that‚Äôs an installation error. This falls 100% under the contractor‚Äôs workmanship warranty. A reputable roofer will come out, fix their mistake, and repair related damage at no cost to you.

This is exactly why choosing a stable, local company is so important. With roughly 2,205 roofing businesses in Arizona, you need a contractor who will actually be around to answer the phone in five years. You can see more details on the state's roofing market in this IBISWorld industry report.

What to Look for in the Fine Print

When a roofer hands you their warranty, don‚Äôt just look at the number of years. Dig a little deeper for these details that reveal the true value of their promise:

  • Coverage Length: How long does the workmanship warranty last? One year is the absolute minimum. Top-tier contractors often stand behind their work for 5, 10, or even 25 years.
  • Transferability: If you sell your home, can the warranty be transferred to the new owner? This is a huge selling point and adds real value to your property.
  • Exclusions: What voids the warranty? Be aware of common exclusions like damage from someone walking on the roof, unapproved alterations (like adding a satellite dish), or storm damage classified as an "Act of God."

How Long Does a Roof Replacement Take?

This is the big one, and the honest answer is: it depends. For a standard, single-family home in Tucson, you're typically looking at 2 to 5 days for a full replacement. But that's just a ballpark.

  • The Materials: Laying asphalt shingles is a much faster process than setting individual clay tiles, which demands a lot more precision and care.
  • Monsoon Surprises: We all know how quickly a monsoon storm can roll in. Pros will never tear off more of a roof than they can make watertight before packing up for the day. A sudden downpour can easily add a day to the schedule for safety reasons.
  • Hidden Damage: Sometimes, we don't know what's under the old roof until it's gone. If we find rotted decking or busted trusses, those structural issues have to be fixed before the new roof goes on. That adds time.
